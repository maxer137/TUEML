Q1#

In the notes, you have seen that ridge regression corresponds to solving $$ \hat{\beta}_{\text{ridge}}#

\arg\min_{\beta\in\mathbb{R}^p} \left{ |y-X\beta|^2+\lambda|\beta|^2 \right}.
\[ We now consider a probabilistic view of linear regression and show that it leads to the same solution. Define the dataset as $X = \{ x_{1:n}\}$ and $y = \{y_{1:n}\}$, and assume the following model: \]
y_i = x_i^T\beta + \varepsilon_i, \qquad \varepsilon_i \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim}\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2),
\[ or equivalently, \]
y_i\mid x_i,\beta \sim \mathcal N(x_i^T\beta,\sigma^2).
\[ Assume also the prior \]
\beta \sim \mathcal N(0,\sigma_p^2 I_p).
\[ We define the MAP estimator by \]
\hat\beta_{\text{MAP}}#

\arg\max_\beta p(\beta\mid X,y). $$

Question: Show that \(\hat\beta_{\text{MAP}}\) is equal to the ridge regression solution for a suitable choice of \(\lambda\).

You may use the following facts:

  • Bayes’ rule:

    \[ p(\beta\mid X,y)\propto p(y\mid X,\beta)p(\beta) \]

  • Conditional independence:

    \[ p(y\mid X,\beta)=\prod_{i=1}^n p(y_i\mid x_i,\beta) \]

  • Applying \(\log\) preserves the maximizer

  • Terms that do not depend on \(\beta\) may be ignored when optimizing over \(\beta\)

Derive the corresponding optimization problem explicitly, and state the relationship between \(\lambda\), \(\sigma^2\), and \(\sigma_p^2\).

Remark: Your score will be based only on your response in the field ” \(\lambda:=\ldots\) “. However, to receive credit, you must still upload your derivation.

==Ans: \(\lambda := \sigma^2/\sigma^2_p\).==

==Solution from the book:==

\[\begin{split} \begin{align} \hat \beta &= \arg \max -\frac12 \left[ \sigma_p^{-2} ||\beta||^2_2 + \sigma^{-2} ||y - X\beta||^2_2 \right] \\ % &= \arg \min \left[ \sigma_p^{-2} ||\beta||^2_2 + \sigma^{-2} ||y - X\beta||^2_2 \right] \\ % &= \arg \min \sigma^2 \left[ \sigma_p^{-2} ||\beta||^2_2 + \sigma^{-2} ||y - X\beta||^2_2 \right] \\ % &= \arg \min \left[ \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma_p^{2}} ||\beta||^2_2 + ||y - X\beta||^2_2 \right] \\ \end{align} \end{split}\]

Q2#

Based on the figure, consider the true regression function marked as \(f^*\) (in blue). Suppose we fit two regression models to different independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) datasets sampled from the true regression function, \(\mathcal{D}_1, \mathcal{D}_2\), resulting in the two red-tinted models in the figure.

For which point \(x_0 \in \mathbb{N}\) do you expect the squared bias of the average predictor relative to \(f^*(x_0)\) to be the largest?

  1. \(x_0 = ...\)

At this point \(x_0\), compute:

  1. The sample mean of the predictions \(f_{i}\left(x_0\right)\).

  2. The squared bias relative to \(f^*\left(x_0\right)\).

  3. The variance of the predictors at \(x_0\) (note that we use in this lecture the biased sample variance, as it is computed by numpy).

==Answers:==

  1. x_0=21

  3. f1 = 11, f2=9, f*=7. -> sample mean 10

  4. \(bias^2\) = \((7-10)^2=9\)

  5. variance = \(\frac{1}{2}((10 - 11)^2 + ((10-9)^2))=1\)

Q3 - Cross-Validation Folds#

Run student_starter.py. It loads two datasets, “regression_iid” and “forecasting”, and visualizes three fold procedures: A, B, and C.

The forecasting dataset is time-ordered: the model uses \(x=y_t\) to predict \(y=y_{t+1}\). The goal is thus to predict the next value from the current value.

Question 1: Match each procedure to the correct description.

Descriptions:

  1. Random split

  2. Systematic interleaved split

  3. Train on earlier samples and test on later ones.

Answers:

  1. Procedure A is description: 1, 2, or 3.

  2. Procedure B is description: 1, 2, or 3.

  3. Procedure C is description: 1, 2, or 3.

Question 2

For regression_iid, the rows may be treated as independent samples with no meaningful time order.

Which fold procedure(s) would you use to obtain a reliable MSE estimate? Select all that apply: A, B, C.

Question 3

Which fold procedure(s) would you use to obtain a reliable MSE estimate? Select all that apply: A, B, C.

==Answers==

  1. A -> 1, B -> 2, C -> 3

  2. A, B

  3. C
